Change Management means controlled process for proposing, reviewing, approving, implementing, and validating a change. Its practical job is that it reduces unintended outages and security regressions when systems or settings are modified. Mechanically, it records the request, evaluates risk and approval, applies the change in a planned window, and verifies the result afterward. Troubleshooting Methodology means structured step-by-step process for diagnosing and resolving technical issues. Its practical job is that it follows defined stages to identify symptoms, test causes, implement fixes, and verify normal operation. Mechanically, it defines a sequence of observe, hypothesize, test, and verify so problems are isolated without random changes. Notice the pattern: the exam target here is not memorizing isolated labels, but recognizing which part of the system is responsible for speed, persistence, coordination, communication, or control. When two concepts seem similar, ask three questions: where does the data live, who acts on it, and what changes if this part fails. That framing turns vocabulary into diagnosis.

## 4. Key distinctions and common confusions
- Change Management vs Patch Management — Change Management: Controlled process for proposing, reviewing, approving, implementing, and validating a change. Patch Management: Process for testing, deploying, and verifying software updates.
- Troubleshooting Methodology vs Tabletop Exercise — Troubleshooting Methodology: Structured step-by-step process for diagnosing and resolving technical issues. Tabletop Exercise: Scenario-based drill used to test readiness and coordination.
- Purpose vs mechanism — be able to separate what Change Management is for from how it actually performs that job.
- Recognition vs diagnosis — knowing the term name is not enough; know what evidence would make you pick it in a scenario.
